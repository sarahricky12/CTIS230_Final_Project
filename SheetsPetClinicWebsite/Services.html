Wellness Exams & Vaccines

Sheets Pet Clinic believes routine preventative care is crucial in keeping your pet healthy and discovering health issues as soon as possible. Dogs and cats should be current on core vaccines. A discussion about your pet’s lifestyle with our medical team may find other diseases that your pet is at increased risk for and vaccinations in addition to core vaccines would be recommended. Any animals going outside should be provided flea and tick control products and inside animals benefit from flea prevention as we can bring fleas inside to our pets. Dogs and cats are susceptible to life threatening disease from heartworm infection spread by mosquitoes. Even indoor animals can be bitten by mosquitos that have come inside by an open window or door.

Dental Care

Managing your pet’s dental health is an important component in helping your pet live a comfortable, healthy life. Dental disease causes not only infection, but pain as well. Animals continue to eat in spite of significant oral pain. Just because a pet is eating does not mean he is not in pain. Cats have some unique, but common dental problems. Feline resorptive lesions allow cats to feel painful stimuli as the protective enamel is missing. These lesions can be quite small and even below the gum line making detection difficult. Cats also get immune-mediated gingivitis and stomatitis causing severe inflammation and pain. During routine yearly physical examination many of these and additional dental issues can be detected and a plan of action created to help your pet live a healthier more comfortable life. Dental cleanings are best done before significant dental disease is present. Cleanings are recommended any time the calculus reaches the gum line causing inflammation and pain. If cleaning is not done at this early stage periodontal disease will advance causing significant pain and ultimately tooth loss. If your pet has advanced dental disease it is in significant pain and the infection in the mouth also affects other parts of the body. Removal of painful and compromised teeth is necessary to alleviate pain and help get infection in the mouth resolved. If your pet has not been seen for an annual exam and you have concerns about their dental health you can schedule a predental exam where your pet’s dental health as well as overall health will be discussed and evaluated. Pet’s cannot be scheduled for a dental procedure without an annual exam or a predental exam. Many animals with significant dental disease or other underlying issues will need to start a predental antibiotic prior to the procedure. Animals over 7 years of age will need preanesthetic blood work performed or on file before anesthesia. It is recommended all animals have preanesthetic blood work prior to the dental procedure, but not required for animals under 7 that appear in good health. However, animals can look healthy and have underlying disease present. Preanesthetic blood work also helps establish a baseline should your pet ever become sick. We can compare the blood work back to the baseline to better see changes.

Senior Care

Making sure our companions are well cared for and comfortable in their golden years is extremely important. Often older animals are seen as getting slower to get up or have less energy because they are “getting older”, but in fact they are in pain. Most dogs develop degenerative joint disease otherwise known as arthritis at some point in their lives. Due to confirmation issues (which can be breed related) some dogs can suffer from this at an early age. We have many tools for managing the pain of arthritis with some promising new treatments as well. Cats suffer from osteoarthritis also and may hide this better than their canine counterparts. We offer routine lab work to help find problems sooner. Early detection allows us to better manage these diseases giving your pet an improved quality of life and more time to enjoy with your family.

Pain Management

We have a stewardship responsibility to make sure we are managing our pets’ comfort and quality of life. Many chronic disease processes like osteoarthritis can be managed well with the tools we have available for control of pain and inflammation. We offer pharmaceutical aid in the form of NSAIDS created for our canines’ sensitive systems. We recommend proven nutritional supplements to help with pain and inflammation as well. Our clinic also uses laser therapy to improve healing on the cellular level as well as Adequan injections to improve synovial fluid. Now we have a new tool which uses monoclonal antibody technology to target a major source of inflammation causing progressive disease. One injection monthly is allowing our arthritic patients to move with more mobility and less pain. This technology is also available for our feline patients that have had fewer options for long term pain control.

Full Lab Services

In-House Pharmacy

When our pets are sick we want to know as soon as possible how best to treat them. Having an In-house pharmacy where we can get results in an hour or less allows us to quickly move forward with a tailored treatment plan for that individual and can often provide us with a diagnosis quickly. We can monitor a patient’s changes throughout the day in real time.

Soft Tissue Removals

Pets commonly have growths especially as they age. Some masses are benign and may not need removal. Some benign masses may need to be removed if your pet is licking or chewing the site or ruptures such as the case of sebaceous cysts. Removing malignant masses when they are small can be a lifesaver. Many tumors are only locally aggressive and slow to metastasize to other areas of the body. If the tumor gets large it may not be possible to remove the tumor in its entirety and it will grow back. Masses should be sent to a pathologist who will look at the tissue microscopically to identify the tumor type, the rate of growth and whether clean surgical margins were attained.

Heartworm Treatment

Our clinic treats dogs that have adult heartworms. The positive dog is started on a month of antibiotics to kill a bacteria associated with the heartworm. They also start on monthly prevention to prevent any new infections from starting. Once they have completed the doxycycline and have received three monthly doses of heartworm preventative they can start the adulticide treatment. An arsenic compound is injected into the deep muscles over the back and the animal is monitored the rest of the day. The dog is sent home on prednisone for inflammation created when the worms start dying and pain medication for the soreness from the injection. It is essential that the dog stays quiet 30 days after the injection. If the heart is beating excessively fast the dog is at increased risk for developing a fatal thromboembolism. The first injection kills nearly all the male worms (they are smaller) and a small percentage of the female worms. This death of only some of the worms allows the body to deal with the dying worm burden and is much safer for the dog. In one month the dog returns for two injections 24 hours apart and will spend the night between the injections. Again the dog will go home on pain medication and prednisone and must stay inactive for 30 more days. The treatment is hard on the dog, but can prevent more serious complications from allowing the adult worms to continue to inhabit the dog. The treatment is also expensive. It is much better to prevent heartworm disease with a monthly preventative than it is to have to inject your pet with arsenic to kill adult heartworms. Heartworms are endemic in the Southeast and spread by mosquitoes. Your dog does not have to have contact with another dog to become infected. Cats are also susceptible to heartworm disease and should be protected with monthly prevention.

Microchipping

All pets should be protected by microchipping. The procedure is quick and can be done without anesthesia (although we do offer a discount for microchipping when animals are asleep for spay/neuter surgery). Most cats do not wear collars with owner identification. Even dogs wearing collars with an ID tag may slip out of their collar when they become lost. Microchipping your pet is your best chance for getting them back if they are found with no identification tag. The pet will receive a tag with the microchip that can be placed on the collar. The information kept by the microchip company can include alternate contacts in case you can’t be reached by one on an ID tag. The microchip tag also lets finders know that your pet is permanently identified should you need to prove ownership.

Look at microchipping like you do house/car insurance. The hope is that you will never need it, but if you do it can be a life saver.

Declawing

There are many personal reasons why an owner may need to have their pet declawed, which may include immunocompromised individuals in the household that cannot risk infection from scratches, people in the home on blood thinners, or pets with a condition that causes self trauma, injury or disease of the nails, or other pets in the household that could be fatally injured by the bacteria on the nails of the cats.

Admission and Discharge

General Information

Surgery is by appointment only. Surgeries are performed Monday-Friday.

Patient check-in on the date of your pet's surgery is from 7:30 am to 8:30 am. Please plan on spending between 30-45 minutes for patient check-in.

Barring extreme heat, we ask that you leave your pet in the car in its carrier until you have completed admission paperwork. If your pet's rabies vaccination is current, you must present the certificate before spay/neuter surgery. If you have no proof of a rabies vaccination on check-in, your pet will receive a one-year rabies vaccination at an additional charge of $10. A rabies vaccination is included in the feral cat spay/neuter fee.

Every pet scheduled for surgery is given a pre-op exam. If our veterinarians see anything that should be brought to your attention, you will be called. Be sure we can reach you easily by phone the day of your pet's surgery. Our veterinarians may recommend pre-anesthetic blood screening when a senior pet has surgery for any reason. All animals spayed/neutered receive a tattoo on a small shaved portion of the underbelly. The tattoo is permanent but isn't harmful to your pet. It is not an extra incision. Early pick-up is 3:00-4:00 pm. Late pick-up 4:30-5:00 pm.

You will pick up your pet on the same day of surgery unless you schedule otherwise. If a surgical patient stays at the clinic overnight, a boarding fee will apply.

Dogs

Dogs brought to Sheets Pet Clinic must be on a leash or in a carrier commercially designed for a pet. Clearly label your carrier. We will keep your carrier at the clinic and your dog will be presented to you at patient discharge in his/her carrier.

At check-in, take your dog’s leash with you, but remember it when you come back to the clinic for check-out.

If you are bringing in multiple dogs or puppies for surgery, they must be admitted in separate carriers in order to be discharged. Even animals that normally get along can get agitated and aggressive as they recover from the anesthesia used during surgery. On discharge: one patient per carrier.

All carriers should be in working order with doors that lock securely. Cardboard boxes, laundry baskets, plastic totes, and home-made carriers are not acceptable.

Please walk your dog prior to entering the clinic so that they have a chance to eliminate.

Cats

Cats should be transported to the clinic in a carrier commercially designed for a pet. Clearly label your carrier. We will keep your carrier at the clinic and your cat will be presented to you at patient discharge in his/her carrier.

If you are bringing in multiple cats or kittens for surgery, they must be brought in separate carriers in order to be discharged. Even animals that normally get along can get agitated and aggressive as they recover from the anesthesia used during surgery. On discharge: one patient per carrier

All carriers should be in working order with doors that lock securely. Cardboard boxes, laundry baskets, plastic totes, and home-made carriers are not acceptable.

Feral Cats

Feral cats must be brought in for surgery in a fully functional humane humane trap. You may leave a carrier for post-operative placement and recovery if you choose. Clearly label that carrier. Bring a towel to place in or over the trap or carrier for transport home.

Bring one carrier per feral cat for discharge, even if they were admitted in one humane trap. Even animals that normally get along can get agitated and aggressive as they recover from the anesthesia used during surgery. On discharge: one patient per carrier or trap.

We loan out humane animal traps for a refundable fee. If the trap is unreturned, or returned broken, we cannot refund your deposit.

Social strays do not qualify for the feral rate. If our staff determines that the cat brought in as a feral is in fact social, you will be charged the normal public rate. For an explanation of the difference between a feral and social stray cat, please visit Alley Cat Allies.

Since we are committed to ending homeless pet overpopulation, pregnant feral cats will be spayed.

All feral cats will receive an ear tip and tattoo to indicate they have been altered and vaccinated.

Pre-Op Care Guidelines

Your pet should sleep indoors overnight the evening before his/her surgery.

The night before surgery, remove all food from adult pets (dogs and cats one year or older) no later than midnight. Pets may have water available overnight, and your pet may have a lap or two of water in the morning of the surgery.

No guzzling!

Removing food and water lessens chances that your adult pet will vomit and aspirate during surgery. Inform the front desk at check-in if your adult pet has eaten the morning of his/her surgery.

Puppies and kittens under four months of age may have access to food the evening before surgery, and they have a small amount to eat until 6 am the morning of the surgery.

For example, a puppy can have 1/4 his/her regular morning meal of kibble before 6 am the morning of the surgery. A kitten may have a tablespoon of canned food before 6 am the morning of the surgery.

Bring your pet’s current vaccination record if you have one. Bring his/her current rabies certificate if you have one. Present these at check-in. A rabies tag is not proof of rabies vaccination. If you have no proof of a rabies vaccination, your pet (if over 16 weeks of age) will receive a one-year rabies vaccination at the time of surgery at an expense of $10.

The veterinarian will perform pre-op exam to ensure your pet is eligible for surgery. If your pet is ill or has too much food in his/her stomach, or if the vet has concerns about any other condition that might put your pet at risk under anesthesia, we will ask that you postpone surgery until the illness or condition is resolved.

Post-Op Care Guidelines

Dogs

All postoperative patients must be kept indoors for 7 days. Discourage any running or jumping and limit stair climbing as much as possible. Outdoor dogs may stay in a clean, warm garage if you are able to watch him/her closely there. Keep your postoperative dog quiet and give him/her comfort.

Please leash-walk your dog for 7 days. Do not allow any free roaming.

Check your dog's mucous membrane (gum) color when you arrive home. The mucous membrane color should be a pale pink to red. More importantly, the color must quickly return to normal after applied pressure to the gums (above the canine tooth). Check your dog's color throughout the evening to verify the mucous membrane color returns to normal.

Offer water immediately after returning home. Provide the water in small amounts throughout the evening, watching for vomiting. If your dog takes a large amount of water, he/she will likely vomit. If after 2-3 hours of offering ice chips or small bowls of water there is no vomiting, you may move to free-choice water. Small dogs and puppies 6 months and younger may eat dry food after 10pm, if they are not vomiting water. Resume normal feeding the day after surgery. Generally, a lack of appetite is normal for the first 24 hours post-op. However, your dog must be drinking water. If he/she shows no interest in food or water after 24 hours, contact the clinic. Also contact the clinic if your dog continues to vomit water after 12 hours.

Check your dog's incision daily for redness, swelling, discharge or wound gaping. Some redness and swelling is normal. Contact the clinic if it looks excessive to you. Contact the clinic about any discharge or opening of the incision.

Do not clean the incision - you may introduce infection. The incision needs to remain dry. If it becomes dirty, flush the incision with Saline Solution (contact lens saline solution). Do not bathe your dog for at least 7 days.

Some discomfort 24-36 hours post-op is normal. Your dog should continually improve. He/she should feel better every day.

Your dog has buried sutures, so there is no need for suture removal.

Male dogs remain virile for 3-4 weeks after surgery. Keep them confined.

Female dogs will remain in heat for 7-10 days even after spay surgery. If your pet was in heat do not allow her near any intact male dogs. Do Not trust that a fence will keep a male dog away. She should be watched carefully.

If you feel your dog is in need of pain-relieving medication, please contact the clinic administering any medicines. Some over-the-counter medications can be very dangerous to your dog.

Cats

All postoperative patients must be kept indoors for 7 days. Feral cats may be very stressed to stay inside and would be better off being released into their familiar surroundings the day following surgery. But, if a cat is released it will be hard to help it if there are any surgical complications. So, if you have a small room indoors (like a bathroom) and the cat will eat daily it should stay inside during the postoperative period.

Female cats should be monitored for normal urination after surgery. This may be easier to accomplish if the cat is isolated with its own litter box until it passes urine. The ureters, which take urine from the kidneys to the bladder, lie close to the base of the uterus. It is an extremely rare complication for the ureters to become compromised in the removal of the uterus, but not impossible. Therefore monitoring female cats post operatively for normal urination is advised. Pregnancy at the time of surgery raises this risk, but is still an extremely rare occurrence.

Check your cat's mucous membrane (gum) color when you arrive home. The mucous membrane color should be a pale pink to dark pink. More importantly, the color should return after removing pressure applied to the gums (above the canine tooth) within 1-2 seconds. Check your cat's color throughout the evening to verify mucous membrane color return.

Offer water immediately after returning home. If your cat is not vomiting, you may offer dry food after 8pm. Resume normal feeding the day after surgery. Generally, a lack of appetite is normal for the first 24 hours post-op. However, your cat must be drinking water. If he/she shows no interest in food or water after 24 hours, contact the clinic. Contact the clinic if your cat continues to vomit water after 12 hours.

Check your cat's incision daily for redness, swelling, discharge or wound gaping. Some redness and swelling is normal. Many female cats will produce a lot of granulation tissue in response to the internal sutures. This tissue is very firm and usually in the area just at the incision. If there is soft swelling it could be an accumulation of tissue fluid (a seroma) that can usually be managed at home, but we would prefer to check the incision to make sure everything is ok. Surgical rechecks are free. Contact the clinic about any discharge or opening of the incision.

Do not clean the incision - you may introduce infection. The incision needs to remain dry. If it becomes dirty, flush the incision with Wound Saline Solution (available at most drug and grocery stores). Do not bathe your cat for at least 7 days.

Some discomfort 24-36 hours post-op is normal. Your cat should continually improve. He/she should feel better every day.

Your female cat has buried sutures, so there is no need for suture removal. Male cats do not require sutures.

Male cats remain virile for 3-4 weeks after surgery. Keep them confined.

If you feel your cat is in need of additional pain-relieving medication, please contact the clinic before administering any medicines. Some over-the-counter medications can be very dangerous for your cat.

Rabbits

Food and water should be made available once the rabbit is fully awake. Your rabbit should be fully awake when it is picked up. Keep the rabbit in a clean area to help prevent infection at the surgery site. Monitor the incision daily for any drainage, swelling, or opening. Monitor your rabbit carefully to make sure it is not chewing on the incision site. If they are chewing at the incision they will need a small comfortable e-collar to wear that is rabbit safe. The incisions should be healed completely by 10-14 days after surgery.

Feral Cats

Feral cats must be kept indoors in a small room for 7 days post-operatively. Cats may be frantic for a short period of time, but most will look for a location in which they feel secure and settle there. Place the post-operative carrier in the room, open the door and leave the room.If housing for 7 days is not possible, they must be kept inside at least over night before releasing.Food can be dropped into the trap

Provide a small box or plastic container in a corner/out of the way area without a lid from which you can visually observe the cat.

Line it with clean towel for the cat.

Remember that cats prefer to just fit in their bed.

Kept in large crates, cats will often sleep in the litter box creating an opportunity for infection.

Do not leave the cat carrier in the room during the recovery period. When you are ready to release the cat, replace the bed/box in the room with the carrier with the carrier door open and a towel inside. Put the carrier out of the way and near where the cat has been sleeping. Move in and out of the room - do so even if the cat is in the carrier - several times, moving gradually closer to the carrier. When you can get very close to the carrier without the cat moving out of it, close the carrier door.

Feral cats will huddle in a small area. Signs of postoperative problems will not be obvious.

Keep cats on white or light-colored towels so you can see any external bleeding.

Watch food intake closely - lack of interest in food, especially after days with a good appetite can signal a problem.

Clean the litter box often - once daily at a minimum. The cat should urinate at least once daily. Lack of urine production, especially in female cats, can indicate a problem

Become familiar with the cat's 'energy level'. In most cases, a feral cat will not move when you are nearby; however, he/she will be watching closely.

If the cat's attention to you when you are nearby seems less energetic or his/her eyes seem less bright/attentive, contact the clinic for support.

If you can touch the cat (formerly untouchable) and the cat feels cool to the touch, (especially female cats) he/she could be in trouble.

If the cat's third eyelid is visible (and does not move back after awaking, etc), this is also a sign of distress.

Feral Cat Spay/Neuter Guidelines

Feral cats are cats that have reverted back to a wild state (do not allow people to handle them). They are not social stray cats. Feral cats must come in a humane trap to receive the feral cat rate. Feral cats that come in carriers or crates will be charged the regular spay/neuter rates. This is because feral cats receive an injection through the trap bars to sedate them so they can be anesthetized for surgery. We have trap dividers to make the space in the trap smaller so they can be sedated easier with less stress to the cat. Feral cats must be ear tipped for identification as a sterilized cat to receive the feral cat rate. Any cats that are not ear tipped do not receive the feral cat rate. The removal of the tip of the ear is done while the cat is under anesthesia for surgery and allows people from a distance to see that the cat has already been fixed. Once trapped the cat trap should be covered with a sheet or towel to remove visual stimulation that will make the cat more reactive and taken to a quiet safe area until transported to the clinic. Many cats can become quite agitated in the trap and will run against the door with great force or try to force their way under the door. Some of these cats will work so hard to get out of the trap that they can create bloody skinned places on their face or feet. These places will heal and the cat should not be released until it has been altered and vaccinated for rabies. Although rabies is the only vaccine required by law, it is highly recommended to vaccinate for panleukopenia. Panleukopenia is a parvovirus of cats with almost 100% fatality rate if infected. It can persist in the environment for years. Panleukopenia outbreaks in shelters cause widespread death and often force shelters to depopulate and shut down for a period of time. Panleukopenia is the “P” in the FVRCP vaccine and protection can be achieved by one dose of a modified live vaccine in felines over 4 months of age. If you trap a cat that is nursing kittens please tell the desk staff as these cats are prioritized for early surgery time so that they can quickly be released back to their kittens. Normally kittens can go 24-36 hours without mom. It is recommended that feral cats remain in a comfortable climate the night after surgery as they do not regulate their body temperature normally after surgery. The exception would be a nursing mom who can be released once anesthesia is completely worn off. You should not attempt to handle or move a trapped cat from the trap. If you should get bitten the cat will need to be quarantined for rabies observation for 10 days. If the cat is not quarantined you will need to go to the hospital for rabies post exposure immunoglobulin injections and start rabies vaccines. Rabies is 99% fatal in people. Once a person has clinical signs of rabies it is too late to be saved. Therefore, be very careful when trapping and returning cats so that you do not get bitten.

Boarding


The clinic boards both canines and felines in separate areas. Dogs are walked in a fenced area 2-3 times daily and are fed according to owner instructions. Cats are not taken outside and can be housed in a large cage with a housemate if preferred. Owners should supply the pets food for their stay as most animals have less gastrointestinal upset if they stay on their regular diet. Some animals do not eat well when boarded so it is ok to bring some treats they like to add to their food.
